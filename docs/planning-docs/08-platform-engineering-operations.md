This inventory provides a comprehensive overview of artifacts that define, manage, and operate an Internal Developer Platform (IDP). The goal is to treat the platform as a product with its own user experience, contracts, and lifecycle. The artifacts are organized into 11 sections, each addressing a core aspect of platform engineering and modern operational practice. Emphasis is placed on emerging trends such as platform contracts, AI integration, and composable architectures.

## Current Recommended Inventory: Platform Engineering & Modern Operational Documentation (2026)

### I. Platform Strategy & Product Management Artifacts

| Artifact | Primary Purpose | Key Content / Standard Fields |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Platform Charter** | Formally authorize the platform initiative and define its mandate. | Problem statement, goals, success metrics (e.g., developer productivity, deployment frequency), initial stakeholder list, team structure, platform boundaries (what is in/out of scope), and funding model. |
| **Platform-as-a-Product Vision Document** | Articulate the long-term product goal for the internal developer platform, treating it as a product for internal customers. | Elevator pitch, target personas (developer teams), key differentiators (e.g., self‑service, reduced cognitive load), success metrics (e.g., time‑to‑first‑commit, reduction in support tickets), and quarterly roadmaps aligned with customer feedback loops. Teams that operate platforms as products with quarterly roadmaps and SLAs tied to product management practices are more likely to achieve high impact. |
| **Internal Developer Platform (IDP) Definition** | A curated set of self-service capabilities, built on top of underlying infrastructure, that allows developers to complete common tasks—provisioning environments, configuring observability, and managing deployments. An IDP is the artifact that results from platform engineering. The IDP provides a bottom‑up approach for platform teams to provide self‑service workflows to their users, from Day 0 to Day 2. |
| **Platform Engagement & Adoption Plan** | Document how developer teams will be onboarded, supported, and measured on the platform. | Onboarding workflow, training materials, SLAs for support, feedback collection mechanisms, adoption metrics (e.g., % of teams using self‑service, reduction in infrastructure tickets), and a developer experience (DevEx) measurement framework. |
| **Platform Technical Debt Register** | Track architectural shortcuts, incomplete capabilities, and known deviations from the planned platform that carry future rework cost. | Debt description, affected components, severity/cost estimate of remediation, date of introduction, responsible team, and current status. Prevents silent erosion where undocumented drift gradually invalidates the platform architecture. |

### II. Platform Architecture & Foundational Artifacts

| Artifact | Primary Purpose | Key Content / Standard Fields |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Platform Architecture Blueprint** | High-level design of the IDP, showing how it orchestrates underlying infrastructure, cloud providers, CI/CD systems, and observability stacks. | Component diagram showing the platform orchestrator (e.g., Kratix, Humanitec), IaC engines (Crossplane, Terraform, Pulumi), developer portal (Backstage), and integrations with external systems; data flow diagram for self‑service requests; security boundaries; and extensibility points. |
| **Platform Reference Architecture** | A pre-architected, reusable infrastructure pattern that standardizes and accelerates cloud development. Often documented as part of a "platform landing zone" approach. | Network topology (VPCs, subnets, firewalls), identity and access management (IAM) structure, logging and monitoring baseline, cost management controls, and guardrails for compliance. The Azure Verified Modules for Platform landing zone (ALZ) provide an opinionated approach for deploying and managing core platform capabilities using Bicep or Terraform. |
| **Platform API Contract** (Kratix Promise / Crossplane Composition) | Formal, machine-readable definition of a self-service capability offered by the platform. It is a set of rules and artifacts that describe, in a consumable way, the capabilities provided (service creation, deployment, secrets, logging, database, API gateway, etc.). The contract turns the platform into a reliable, governed, adoptable experience. | Kratix defines a **Promise** as a contract between platform and development teams. It includes: 1) the API schema (what developers can request), 2) the workflow templates (how the platform fulfills the request), and 3) the resource requirements. Crossplane uses **Compositions** and **Claims** to achieve a similar contract. The platform team codifies their organization's policies and opinions (security, compliance) into their as‑a‑Service software supply chain. |
| **Platform Architecture Decision Records (ADR)** | Document the "why" behind significant platform architecture choices, including decisions on orchestration tooling, portal framework, multi‑cloud strategy, and API design. | Each ADR includes context (problem and constraints), decision (the choice made), consequences (positive and negative), alternatives considered, related decisions, and metadata (author, date, status). ADRs are stored in the platform team's repository. |

### III. Core IDP Components Artifacts

Platform engineering teams are increasingly adopting a **composable platform** approach, using modular, reusable components as the foundation for effective platform engineering.

| Artifact | Primary Purpose | Key Content / Standard Fields |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Service Catalog Definition** (Backstage Software Catalog / alike) | A registry of every service, library, API, and resource in the organization. It acts as the single source of truth for what runs where, who owns it, and how it is documented. The catalog restores order to microservices and infrastructure, enabling product teams to ship high-quality code quickly without compromising autonomy. | Defined via YAML entity files with fields: `apiVersion`, `kind` (Component, API, System, Resource, Domain), `metadata` (name, description, labels, annotations), `spec` (owner, lifecycle, type, dependsOn). Relationships between entities (e.g., a Component consumes an API) are machine-readable for dependency analysis. |
| **Software Template Definition** (Scaffolder) | Self-service scaffolding for creating new projects with standardised, secure workflows that reduce friction and support self‑service at scale. Templates are a critical part of platform engineering by offering pre‑configured golden paths. | A YAML definition (`apiVersion: scaffolder.backstage.io/v1beta3`, `kind: Template`) that describes the template, its metadata, and input variables. The template defines actions (e.g., clone template, run a script, create a GitHub repo, register in the catalog). |
| **TechDocs Artifact** (docs‑as‑code) | A documentation generator that renders technical documentation (written in Markdown/MkDocs) inside the developer portal. TechDocs ensures documentation is stored alongside code, versioned, and discoverable. | Documentation source files in the repository (`/docs` folder), a `mkdocs.yml` configuration file, and a CI process that builds and publishes the documentation to cloud storage from which the portal serves it. |
| **Backstage Plugin Configuration & Customization Record** | Document which Backstage plugins are installed, how they are configured, and any custom plugins developed for the organization's unique needs. | Plugin name, version, configuration YAML, integration details (e.g., GitHub, Jenkins, Kubernetes), RBAC rules, and custom plugin source code references. |
| **IDP Extensibility & Integration Map** | Map how the IDP connects to CI/CD pipelines, IaC engines, cloud APIs, security scanners, and other enterprise systems. | Integration points, authentication methods, data flow directions, and expected latency/throughput. For cross‑platform extensibility, the IDP should offer a well-documented integration layer. |

### IV. Golden Paths, Guardrails & Governance Artifacts

Golden paths, guardrails, and safety nets are the three pillars of a well‑governed developer platform. Together, they balance developer autonomy with organizational standards.

| Artifact | Primary Purpose | Key Content / Standard Fields |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Golden Path Template** | A pre‑configured, paved road that provides an end‑to‑end workflow for developers. A golden path is a self‑service template for common tasks that allows for autonomy while providing guardrails that safeguard production environments. | Infrastructure components (Pulumi Components / Terraform Modules) and deployable patterns (Templates) that developers can instantiate with minimal configuration. The golden path encodes security defaults, naming conventions, observability hooks, and compliance checks. Platform engineers must curate a bespoke platform API to offer a compelling platform‑as‑a‑product. |
| **Guardrails Documentation** | Non‑negotiable rules enforced by the platform, often implemented as **Policy as Code**. Guardrails include style checks, structure validation, commit/PR templates, and provenance requirements. | Explicit rules: "Every repository must have a SECURITY.md," "Every service must have a health check endpoint," "Every deployment must pass SAST scanning." Implementation details (e.g., Open Policy Agent (OPA) rules, Pulumi Policy as Code, or CI pipeline checks). |
| **Safety Nets Specification** | Automated recovery and rollback policies that protect production environments from human error or faulty deployments. | Auto‑rollback triggers (e.g., error rate >1%, failed health checks for X seconds), blue‑green deployment configuration, canary analysis thresholds, database migration rollback plan, and automated drift detection between desired and actual state. |
| **Platform Service Level Objectives (SLOs) & Metrics** | Quantitative targets that the platform provides to its users (developer teams), treating the platform as a product. | SLOs: `service creation < 5 minutes`, `deployment API availability 99.9%`, `catalog search latency < 200ms`. Business‑facing metrics: Deployment frequency, lead time for changes, and developer satisfaction scores. |

### V. Cloud Infrastructure & IaC Artifacts

Modern platform engineering advocates for an **API‑first** approach to infrastructure. Tools like Crossplane enable a Kubernetes‑native control plane for infrastructure orchestration.

| Artifact | Primary Purpose | Key Content / Standard Fields |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Infrastructure as Code (IaC) Module Specification** (Azure Verified Modules) | Standards and specifications for creating reusable, trustworthy infrastructure modules, ensuring modules align to standards across languages (Bicep, Terraform). Modules classified as `resource`, `pattern`, or `utility`. Specifications are grouped by IaC language and module classification. |
| **Crossplane Provider Configuration** | Document the Crossplane providers installed in the cluster and configurations cloud provider credentials, region defaults, and feature flags. | Provider name (e.g., `provider-aws`, `provider-azure`, `provider-gcp`), version, package location, credentials secret reference, default region, and configuration YAML. |
| **Crossplane Composition Artifact** | A YAML definition that composes managed resources (e.g., an RDS instance + an S3 bucket) into a single reusable unit, abstracting infrastructure complexity. | `apiVersion: apiextensions.crossplane.io/v1`, `kind: Composition`, `spec` containing composite resource claim (XRC) schema, resources list with base templates and patches, and connection details. |
| **Crossplane Claim Definition** | A namespace‑scoped API that allows application teams to request infrastructure resources without platform team intervention. The claim workflow has four steps: platform team defines Composition → platform team publishes Composite Resource Definition (XRD) → app team creates a Claim → Crossplane reconciles resources. |
| **Terraform/Pulumi Module Registry Inventory** | A catalog of approved, versioned modules that developers can use to provision infrastructure resources. Often stored in a private registry (e.g., Terraform Cloud, Pulumi Registry). | Module name, version, source URL, input variables, output variables, providers required, documentation, and approval status. |

### VI. Platform Orchestration & Delivery Artifacts

The platform orchestrator is the core engine that fulfills self‑service requests.

| Artifact | Primary Purpose | Key Content / Standard Fields |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Kratix Promise Definition** | A machine‑readable contract that defines a self‑service capability, including its API schema, the workflows required to fulfill a request, and the infrastructure resources to be created. Kratix focuses on empowering platform engineers to build better platforms. | Example includes: **Promise.yaml** (API group, version, kind, spec containing `workflows` for request and resource creation, and `resources` defining what infrastructure to orchestrate). |
| **Humanitec Platform Orchestrator Configuration** | Defines how application configurations are dynamically generated and deployed across environments using a rule‑based engine. The orchestrator creates a consistent code representation of the environment and keeps an audit trail of every single change to all systems. | **Definition YAML** includes `application`, `environment`, `resources` (e.g., databases, caches), and `rules` for dynamic configuration. An audit trail of every configuration change is automatically maintained. |
| **KubeVela Application Delivery Workflow** | Declarative application deployment plan that captures and executes full microservice deployments on hybrid environments through an Open Application Model (OAM). KubeVela makes deploying and operating applications across hybrid, multi‑cloud environments easier, faster, and more reliable. | `vela.yaml` or OAM Application Configuration includes `components` (workload types), `traits` (operational features), `workflow steps` (deployment pipeline), and `policies` (override rules). |
| **Fleet Management & GitOps Configuration** (Kratix/KubeVela) | Document how multi‑cluster deployments are managed through GitOps principles, enabling scaling operations across edge sites and multiple environments. Kratix supports scaling operations through fleet management capabilities that embrace and extend GitOps principles, as well as promoting platform co‑creation and autonomy through inner sourcing (“multiplayer mode”). |

## Implementation Guidelines

Implementing these artifacts requires blending classic product management disciplines with modern cloud‑native and platform engineering practices. For many teams, the most valuable starting point is a **platform charter** to align stakeholders and an initial **service catalog** with a few **golden path templates** for the most common developer workflows. The platform charter is particularly important because platform teams are increasingly established as internal providers of reusable services and tools.

The first milestone should focus on measurable value: reduce time‑to‑first‑commit for developers, eliminate tickets for infrastructure requests, and capture baseline metrics to prove ROI. Once the platform delivers a working self‑service capability, the platform team should shift mode to gathering feedback, tracking maturity using the CNCF model, and iterating. By treating the platform as a product, with its own roadmap, SLAs, and customer feedback loops, the platform team can ensure long‑term adoption and impact.

The second‑order artifacts—contracts, guardrails, observability, and maturity assessments—should be introduced incrementally as the platform matures. Policy‑as‑code and automated compliance gating are important to scale safely after initial adoption has been proven.

One of the most important considerations is the **organizational dimension**: Platform engineering is as much about people, process, and policies as it is about technology. The documentation must reflect that this is a socio‑technical endeavor, with artifacts that capture not just architectures but also adoption plans, governance models, and developer feedback loops.
